A.1 Preprocessing

Sentence Segmentation

Definition

This process entails the separation of a paragraph or a document into sentences by detecting where sentences begin and end.

Purpose

The purpose of this NLP task is to enhance machine comprehension of the text before any other NLP processes are done.

Working Principle

Sentences are separated by determining sentence starting and ending points using punctuation symbols such as ., ? and !. Sentence boundary detection uses rules-based approaches, regular expression and machine learning approaches such as CRF and BiLSTM. Abbreviations such as Dr., U.S.A. and e.g. are also considered to avoid wrong sentence split.

Example
# Input: "Dr. Smith visited the U.S.A. yesterday. He loved it!"
# Output: ["Dr. Smith visited the U.S.A. yesterday.", "He loved it!"]
Applications

Document Summarization, Machine Translation, Sentiment Analysis, Legal Documents Analysis, Chatbot Dialogue Management

Advantages

  • It is the basis of many NLP tasks
  • It improves processing of text by splitting sentences
  • It enables the processing of multiple sentences independently

Limitations

  • Mistakenly detects abbreviations as sentence endings
  • Less accuracy for informal text without punctuation
  • Different sentence ending rules in different languages
A.2 Preprocessing

Tokenization

Definition

Tokenization involves splitting text into tokens which are words, subwords or characters to be inputted in NLP models.

Purpose

The purpose of tokenization is to convert continuous text into small units of data that can be processed by computers.

Working Principle

In word tokenization, splitting is done with the help of space and punctuation marks (for example, NLTK, spaCy). Subword tokenization (BPE, WordPiece) splits uncommon or out-of-vocabulary words into subtokens. In character tokenization, each character becomes a token.

Example
# Input: “Tokenization is fundamental!”
# Word-level: ["Tokenization", "is", "fundamental", "!"]
# BPE-level: ["Token", "ization", "is", "fundamental", "!"]
Applications

Search engines, Text classification, Named entity recognition, Speech to text, LLM preprocessing

Advantages

  • The conversion of the text to a form that can be interpreted by the NLP algorithms
  • It helps create the vocabulary of tokens
  • Sub-word tokenization is effective for rare tokens

Limitations

  • The abundance of tokens and out-of-vocabulary in word-level tokenization
  • The length of sequences and loss of the meaning of the word in character-level tokenization
  • Languages have different tokenizations
A.3 Preprocessing

Case Normalization

Definition

The process of transforming all the characters of text into lowercase or uppercase letters.

Purpose

The aim of such process is to reduce vocabulary and make "Apple", "apple", and "APPLE" one word rather than three separate words.

Working Principle

Such task can be performed through text manipulation operations such as text.lower() or text.upper(). In some cases of NLP techniques, there occur cases where truecasing technique is applied and thus certain words retain their initial casing.

Example
# Input: "The Apple CEO announced new Apple products."
# Lowercase: "the apple ceo announced new apple products."
# Truecasing: "The Apple CEO announced new Apple products."
Applications

Text Classification, Sentiment Analysis, Spam Detection, Topic Modeling, Information Retrieval

Advantages

  • Vocabulary reduction
  • Performance efficiency
  • Computability

Limitations

  • Information loss
  • Difficult named entity recognition
  • Loss of emotions or nuance
A.4 Preprocessing

Stop-word Removal

Definition

Stopwords removal is a technique to filter out useless words that does not add any meaning to the sentences.

Purpose

To reduce sentence length and ensure only meaningful words are computed.

Working Principle

We can filter out the words that appear in the list of pre-defined stopwords like "the", "is", "at", "which" and so forth.

Example
# Input: "The quick orange fox jumps over the lazy cat."
# Output after stopwords removal: ["quick", "orange", "fox", "jumps", "lazy", "cat"]
Applications

Document Clustering, Keyword Extraction, Text Summarization, etc.

Advantages

  • Less number of words
  • Fast text processing
  • Significant words only are taken into account

Limitations

  • Important words like "not" can get filtered out altering the meaning of the sentences
A.5 Preprocessing

Stemming

Definition

This is the procedure whereby words are devoid of prefixes and suffixes to arrive at the base form of the word; it is through this procedure that words are created that cannot even be found in dictionaries.

Purpose

The purpose/objective of stemming is to make words fall under one category such that the number of vocabulary words is reduced.

Working Principle

This is the rule based approach and contains Porter Stemming, Snowball Stemming and Lancaster Stemming. All these approaches remove the common prefixes/suffixes.

Example
# Input: ["running", "runs", "runner", "ran"]
# Porter Output: ["run", "run", "runner", "ran"]
# Lancaster Output: ["run", "run", "run", "ran"]
Applications

Information Retrieval, Search Engines, Text Mining, Spam Filtering, Keyword Extraction

Advantages

  • Quick and easy
  • Reduces the vocabulary
  • Doesn’t require any dictionary
  • Useful in information retrieval

Limitations

  • Creates new words which are not present in the dictionary
  • Over stems or under stems words
  • Does not understand the meanings of words
A.6 Preprocessing

Lemmatization

Definition

Converting words into their dictionary word forms.

Purpose

Lemmatization is used for getting dictionary words so as to not lose meaning.

Working Principle

Lemmatization works on the basis of information from lexicons such as WordNet with POS tagging.

Example
# Input: ["running", "better", "geese", "was"]
# Output: ["run", "good", "goose", "be"]
Applications

Sentiment analysis, chatbots, question answering, machine translation, clinical NLP, legal documents

Advantages

  • Gives accurate dictionary words
  • Helps for irregular words
  • Maintains meaning of the word
  • Better than stemming

Limitations

  • It takes more time than stemming
  • Needs POS tagging
  • Computationally intensive
  • Does not work well for domain specific words
A.7 Preprocessing

Noise Removal

Definition

The definition of this operation refers to the process where unnecessary components of texts like HTML tags, URLs, special characters, emojis, and whitespaces are eliminated prior to NLP modeling.

Purpose

Here, the objective is to improve the quality of the text by eliminating any unnecessary components affecting NLP modeling.

Working Principle

There are several techniques through which unnecessary components of the text can be eliminated without removing any necessary components of the text.

Example
# Input: "Check out https://example.com! 🎉 Great deal!!!"
# Output: "Check out Great deal"
Applications

Social media analysis, web scraping, OCR post-processing, log analysis, preprocessing pipelines

Advantages

  • Improves the quality of the input text
  • Eliminates all superfluous elements/noise
  • Provides consistency of the text for further processing
  • Can improve the performance of the models

Limitations

  • May remove some necessary parts, like emojis
  • Removal of unnecessary parts may result in removing necessary patterns
  • Certain rules are needed for each case
A.8 Preprocessing

Text Cleaning

Definition

This relates to the manner through which various forms of text preprocessing techniques are used in transforming the unstructured text to a structured one to facilitate the use of NLP algorithms.

Purpose

The purpose of using text preprocessing techniques is to ensure that there is improvement in quality of the text in terms of its consistency.

Working Principle

The principle of this technique involves various text preprocessing techniques such as encoding conversion, removal of HTML, expansion of contractions, case normalization, noise removal, spelling correction and whitespace normalization.

Example
# Raw: "I can't wait 4 the new iPhone!!! 😍 Visit: apple.com"
# Cleaned: "i cannot wait for the new iphone visit apple com"
Applications

Sentiment analysis, chatbots, document classification etc.

Advantages

  • Enhances the consistency and quality of the text
  • Ensures the formation of a structured pipeline of the pre-processing technique
  • Customizable according to industry needs
  • Basis of NLP processes

Limitations

  • Loss of valuable information during cleansing process
  • Selection of the right text preprocessing techniques
  • Order of text preprocessing is critical
  • Time consuming if applied to large amounts of text
A.9 Feature Engineering

Bag of Words (BoW)

Definition

A feature extraction technique used in NLP that represents text by counting the frequency of words in a document while ignoring their order or grammatical structure.

Purpose

The purpose is to convert unstructured text into a numerical format suitable for machine learning algorithms.

Working Principle

A vocabulary of all unique words across a text corpus is constructed. Each document is then represented as a vector where the length is the vocabulary size, and each entry is the count of how many times a word appears in that document.

Example
# Input: "I love NLP" and "NLP is great"
# Vocabulary: {"I": 0, "love": 1, "NLP": 2, "is": 3, "great": 4}
# Output Vectors:
# "I love NLP" -> [1, 1, 1, 0, 0]
# "NLP is great" -> [0, 0, 1, 1, 1]
Applications

Text classification, Spam detection, Information retrieval

Advantages

  • Very simple and intuitive to implement
  • Effective for text classification on small datasets
  • Keeps word count details

Limitations

  • Disregards word order and context
  • Suffers from the curse of dimensionality
  • Results in highly sparse vectors
A.10 Feature Engineering

N-Grams

Definition

A contiguous sequence of n items (words or characters) from a given text sample, used to capture local word order and contextual information.

Purpose

To preserve some sequential information and local context that unigram models lose, improving representation quality.

Working Principle

Extract all n-length sliding windows from tokenized text. Unigram (n=1), Bigram (n=2), Trigram (n=3).

Example
# Text: "The quick orange fox"
# Bigrams (n=2): ["The quick", "quick orange", "orange fox"]
# Trigrams (n=3): ["The quick orange", "quick orange fox"]
Applications

Language models, Sentence completion, Spell checkers

Advantages

  • Captures local word order/phrases
  • Simple concept and implementation
  • Improves classification accuracy over simple bag-of-words

Limitations

  • Exponential growth of features with higher n
  • Data sparsity increases significantly
  • Misses long-range dependencies in sentences
A.11 Feature Engineering

Term Frequency (TF)

Definition

A statistical measure indicating how frequently a term occurs in a document, normalized by document length.

Purpose

To quantify the importance of a word within a specific document, assuming frequent words are more relevant.

Working Principle

Normalized TF: count(t,d) / total words in d. Log normalization: log(1 + count(t,d)) to dampen high frequencies.

Example
# Doc: "NLP is great. NLP is the future." (10 words)
TF("NLP") = 2/10 = 0.2
TF("is") = 2/10 = 0.2
TF("future") = 1/10 = 0.1
Applications

Information retrieval, Keyword density analysis, Document classification vector input

Advantages

  • Simple and intuitive
  • Computationally efficient
  • Foundation for TF-IDF

Limitations

  • Favors common words
  • No corpus-level info
  • Sensitive to doc length
A.12 Feature Engineering

Inverse Document Frequency (IDF)

Definition

A measure of how rare or common a word is across an entire corpus, used to downweight frequent words and upweight rare terms.

Purpose

To identify words that are distinctive to specific documents, helping distinguish between documents.

Working Principle

IDF(t,D) = log(N / df(t)) where N = total documents, df(t) = documents containing term t. Higher IDF = more distinctive.

Example
# Corpus: 1000 documents
"the" in 990 docs → IDF = log(1000/990) ≈ 0.01
"quantum" in 5 docs → IDF = log(1000/5) ≈ 5.3
Applications

Information retrieval, Vocabulary key term weighting, Search engine ranking algorithms

Advantages

  • Identifies discriminative words
  • Reduces common word impact
  • Grounded in information theory

Limitations

  • Assumes term independence
  • Sensitive to corpus size
  • Cannot handle new docs
A.13 Feature Engineering

TF-IDF

Definition

A statistical metric that scales word frequencies to reflect how important a word is to a document relative to a collection of documents (corpus).

Purpose

To extract features that give more weight to rare, informative words, preventing very frequent words from dominating the models.

Working Principle

Product of TF and IDF. Term Frequency (TF) calculates occurrence in document. Inverse Document Frequency (IDF) measures global rarity.

Example
# Document: "NLP is great. NLP is the future."
# TF of "NLP" = 2 / 10 = 0.2
# If "NLP" appears in 100/1000 documents, IDF("NLP") = log(1000/100) = 1.0
# TF-IDF("NLP") = 0.2 * 1.0 = 0.2
Applications

Search engine retrieval, Topic modeling, Document similarity

Advantages

  • Simple, fast, and highly interpretable
  • Effectively downweights common words/stopwords
  • Good baseline for information retrieval and similarity

Limitations

  • Disregards word order and context
  • Fails to capture synonymy or semantic meaning
  • Computationally constrained by corpus size
A.14 Representation

One-Hot Encoding

Definition

A basic word representation technique where each word in the vocabulary is mapped to a sparse binary vector of size V (vocabulary size).

Purpose

To convert words into numerical representations that ML algorithms can read, treating each word as an independent category.

Working Principle

Each unique word is assigned a specific index. The word's vector has a 1 at its index and 0 everywhere else.

Example
# Vocabulary: ["cat", "dog", "bird"]
# "cat" -> [1, 0, 0]
# "dog" -> [0, 1, 0]
# "bird" -> [0, 0, 1]
Applications

Input layering in simple models, categorical labeling

Advantages

  • Extremely simple and easy to understand
  • Does not assume any misleading order relationships
  • Efficient for very small vocabularies

Limitations

  • Serious curse of dimensionality for large vocabularies
  • No semantic relationship or similarity captured between words
  • High sparsity leads to memory inefficiency
A.15 Representation

Word Embeddings

Definition

Dense, low-dimensional vector representations of words where semantically similar words are mapped to nearby points in vector space.

Purpose

To capture semantic and syntactic relationships between words, enabling models to generalize based on meaning rather than co-occurrence.

Working Principle

Neural networks learn to predict words from context. Similar words have similar vector directions. Analogies via vector arithmetic: king - man + woman ≈ queen.

Example
# "king" -> [0.2, -0.5, 0.8, ..., 0.1] (300-dim)
# Cosine similarities
sim("king", "queen") ≈ 0.75
sim("king", "apple") ≈ 0.05
# Vector arithmetic: king - man + woman ≈ queen
Applications

Semantic search, Sentiment analysis, Neural machine translation, Recommendation engines

Advantages

  • Captures semantic relationships
  • Dense vectors (low dim)
  • Enables analogies

Limitations

  • Polysemy problem (fixed single vector per word)
  • Static representation ignores surrounding sentence context
  • Requires large corpora to train effectively
A.16 Representation

Word2Vec

Definition

A shallow neural network model (Google, 2013) that learns word embeddings by predicting words from their context (CBOW) or context from words (Skip-gram).

Purpose

To produce high-quality, dense word representations that capture semantic and syntactic relationships efficiently.

Working Principle

CBOW: Predicts target from context (faster, frequent words). Skip-gram: Predicts context from target (rare words). Uses negative sampling.

Example
# Training: "The cat sat on the mat"
# Skip-gram (window=2)
Input: "sat" → Predict: ["The", "cat", "on", "the"]
# Result: "cat" and "dog" have high cosine similarity
Applications

Search query expansions, Recommendation systems, Transfer learning

Advantages

  • Efficient to train on large corpora
  • Captures semantic + syntactic similarities
  • Vector arithmetic enables analogical reasoning

Limitations

  • Single vector per word (fails for polysemy)
  • No subword information (OOV issue)
  • Context-independent during inference
A.17 Representation

FastText

Definition

An extension of Word2Vec (Facebook AI, 2016) representing words as bags of character n-grams, enabling embeddings for rare and Out-Of-Vocabulary (OOV) words.

Purpose

To overcome Word2Vec's OOV limitation by incorporating subword information, particularly effective for morphologically rich languages.

Working Principle

Each word is represented as itself plus a bag of character n-grams. The word vector is the sum of its n-gram representations.

Example
# Word: "university"
Subwords (n=3): <un, uni, niv, ive, ver, ers, rsi, sit, ity, ty>
Vector = sum(subword vectors)
# OOV: "universities" (shares n-grams, can be represented!)
Applications

Text classification in morphologically rich languages, Out-of-vocabulary word representation, Spelling correction

Advantages

  • Handles OOV (Out-Of-Vocabulary) words smoothly
  • Great performance on morphologically rich languages
  • Very fast implementation in C++

Limitations

  • Worse on pure semantic similarity tasks compared to Word2Vec
  • Significantly larger model file size
  • Introduces noise for extremely short words
A.18 Representation

Contextual Embeddings

Definition

Dynamic word representations where the vector for a word changes based on its surrounding sentence context, solving the polysemy problem.

Purpose

To generate context-dependent representations capturing different meanings of the same word in different sentences.

Working Principle

ELMo: use BiLSTM. BERT: use Masked LM + Next Sentence Prediction. GPT: use autoregressive Transformer decoder. Calculates context via self-attention.

Example
# Sentence 1: "I sat by the river bank."
# Sentence 2: "I deposited money at the bank."
# "bank" has DIFFERENT vectors! Cosine Similarity ≈ 0.3
Applications

Named entity recognition, Question answering, Sentiment analysis, Coreference resolution

Advantages

  • Solves polysemy (contextualized representations)
  • Provides state-of-the-art results across general NLP tasks
  • Enables modern transfer learning paradigm

Limitations

  • Extremely computationally expensive to train and run
  • Very large model sizes
  • Suffer from a "black-box" interpretability problem
A.19 Language Models

Statistical Language Models

Definition

Language models that estimate the probability of word sequences using statistical frequency methods based on training corpora.

Purpose

To predict the next word in a sequence or evaluate the likelihood of given text, forming the classical foundation for speech recognition.

Working Principle

Uses N-gram models: P(w_n | w_1...w_{n-1}) ≈ count(w_1...w_n) / count(w_1...w_{n-1}). Incorporates smoothing techniques (e.g., Laplace, Kneser-Ney).

Example
# Bigram model: P("learning" | "machine") = count("machine learning") / count("machine")
# If "machine learning" = 50, "machine" = 200: P = 50/200 = 0.25
Applications

Speech recognition, Machine translation, Spell checkers, Pinyin input keyboards

Advantages

  • Highly simple and mathematically interpretable
  • Extremely fast inference speeds
  • Can work reasonably on very small datasets

Limitations

  • Severe data sparsity (zeros for unseen sequences)
  • No semantic representation or general understanding of words
  • Exponential storage requirements as n increases
A.20 Language Models

Neural Language Models

Definition

Language models using neural networks (RNNs, LSTMs, GRUs) to learn distributed representations and predict word probabilities dynamically.

Purpose

To overcome data sparsity and generalization limitations of statistical n-gram models by learning continuous spaces.

Working Principle

An RNN/LSTM processes text sequentially, maintaining a hidden state. Word embedding layers map words to dense vectors, outputting softmax probabilities.

Example
# Input: "The cat sat on the..."
# Embedding: "The" -> [0.1, -0.3, ...]
# LSTM processes sequentially, outputting: P("mat") = 0.4, P("chair") = 0.2
Applications

Text generation, Advanced spell correction, Sentiment classification, Predictive text/autocomplete

Advantages

  • Models longer-range context dependencies than n-grams
  • Generalizes well to unseen combinations of words
  • Learns dense distributed word representations

Limitations

  • Sequential processing results in slow training times
  • Suffers from vanishing/exploding gradients over long sequences
  • Limited effective context window in practice
A.21 Language Models

Transformer-Based Representations

Definition

Deep learning representations based on the Transformer architecture (self-attention mechanism) that process entire input sequences in parallel.

Purpose

To capture bidirectional global dependencies and enable large-scale pre-training (transfer learning) for downstream NLP tasks.

Working Principle

Self-Attention: Attention(Q,K,V) = softmax(QK^T/√d_k)V. Positional encodings are added to preserve sequence order information.

Example
# Sentence: "The animal didn't cross the street because it was too tired."
Self-attention allows "it" to attend strongly to "animal" (not "street").
Applications

Search query understanding (Google Search), Chatbots and virtual assistants, Summarization, Contextual classification

Advantages

  • Fully parallelized training (no sequential bottleneck)
  • Captures dependencies regardless of distance in sequence
  • Scales effectively to billions of parameters

Limitations

  • Quadratic O(n^2) complexity with sequence length
  • Massive training hardware compute requirements
  • High memory footprint for very long sequences
