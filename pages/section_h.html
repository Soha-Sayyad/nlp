Section H

Reflection Notes

Critical reflections on learning outcomes, challenges faced, and future applications of Week 1 NLP concepts.

A

Reflection Note A — Soha Sayyad

Roll No. 61 | Concept Cards & Comparisons Lead

Working on this Knowledge Artifact Repository deepened my understanding of how NLP preprocessing and representation techniques form an interconnected pipeline rather than isolated steps. Initially, I viewed tokenization and vectorization as mechanical procedures, but researching their interactions revealed critical dependencies—poor tokenization directly degrades embedding quality, and preprocessing choices must align with the downstream model architecture.

Creating the Word2Vec vs FastText comparison was particularly illuminating. I discovered that the "better" model depends entirely on the use case—Word2Vec excels at semantic relationships in English, while FastText's subword handling makes it indispensable for morphologically rich languages. This nuanced understanding would not have emerged from textbook reading alone.

The workflow diagrams forced me to think systematically about pipeline design. I realized that preprocessing is not one-size-fits-all—a sentiment analysis pipeline must preserve negation words during stop-word removal, while an information retrieval pipeline might prioritize aggressive stemming for speed.

Looking forward, I plan to explore how these Week 1 foundations enable modern LLMs. Understanding that BERT's contextual embeddings are built upon the same tokenization and attention principles we studied makes the field feel accessible rather than magical. I intend to implement these concepts in a mini-project: building a semantic search engine for academic papers using TF-IDF and sentence embeddings.

B

Reflection Note B — Parv Shah

Roll No. 65 | Preprocessing & Workflows Lead

This repository project transformed my perspective on NLP from "using black-box libraries" to "understanding why each step matters." The Stemming vs Lemmatization comparison challenged my assumption that "more accurate is always better"—I now appreciate that stemming's speed advantage makes it the right choice for search engines processing billions of queries, while lemmatization is essential for clinical NLP where "univers" vs "university" could change a diagnosis.

Designing the Complete Preprocessing Pipeline diagram revealed the critical importance of step ordering. I initially placed stop-word removal before tokenization, which would have been impossible. This mistake taught me that pipeline design requires both theoretical knowledge and practical implementation awareness. The order of operations matters significantly—encoding normalization must precede tokenization, and lemmatization requires POS tags that can only be generated from properly tokenized input.

Researching BERT and Transformer architectures for the concept card was the most challenging yet rewarding part. Understanding how self-attention replaces sequential processing with parallel computation helped me grasp why modern NLP is so much faster than RNN-based approaches. The mathematical elegance of the attention mechanism—softmax(QKT/√dk)V—belies its profound impact.

I was particularly struck by the sustainability implications of our research. Learning that training GPT-3 emitted 552 tons of CO₂ made me critically evaluate whether every large model is necessary. The emergence of efficient architectures like Mixture of Experts and model distillation techniques gives me hope that the field is maturing toward responsible innovation.

For future application, I plan to build a multilingual chatbot for rural healthcare in India using FastText embeddings and lightweight Transformer models. This would directly apply our Week 1 knowledge to a real societal need—providing health information in regional languages where medical professionals are scarce.
